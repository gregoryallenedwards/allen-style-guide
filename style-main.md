# Writing Style Guide

*Hand this to any tool or collaborator before they touch a piece of writing. The master test for every sentence: would a thoughtful person have written this, or did a machine assemble it to sound like one?*

---

## The voice target: Huxley crossed with Gladwell

Neither alone is right. The goal is a specific blend.

**From Huxley:** the long, supple sentence that carries a real idea without collapsing under it; erudition worn lightly; a faint cool detachment even when the subject is warm; the willingness to reach for a large claim and state it quietly rather than sell it; timelessness, meaning no reference that will need a footnote in a century.

**From Gladwell:** the concrete scene as the way into an idea; the specific number and the specific detail; the pull-back from a particular moment to the pattern behind it; propulsion, the sense that the next sentence is worth getting to; the occasional turn to "you" that puts the reader inside the question.

**The blend in practice:** open in something concrete and particular (Gladwell), then let the prose lengthen and deepen as it reaches for what the particular means (Huxley). Ground every abstraction in a thing you could photograph. Earn every large sentence with a small one nearby.

A sentence that achieves the blend:

> *The dome did not seem to rest on the building at all. It seemed to hang from heaven on a golden chain. The effect is achieved with light. A ring of windows runs around the base of the dome, so that the huge weight of stone overhead appears to come loose from the walls beneath it and float.*

Concrete detail first (the windows, the light), then the precise observation it enables (the dome appears to float), then the pull-back: Christians, Muslims, and tourists with no belief at all have all stood under that dome and felt the same thing happen in their chests.

## Hard rules (non-negotiable)

1. **No em dashes. Ever.** Not for asides, not for emphasis, not for trailing thoughts. Use a comma, a colon, a period, or restructure the sentence. This is the single most reliable tell of machine prose.

2. **No reference that dates the writing.** No living celebrities as load-bearing examples, no current events, no technology that will look quaint, no slang. If an example will need explaining in fifty years, find an older one that does the same work. A building from the sixth century is safer than a famous musician.

3. **No proselytizing.** The writing never tells the reader what to believe. It reports, it reasons, it offers. The reader chooses.

4. **Felt experience stays subjective.** When describing something experienced rather than established, mark it as experienced ("a thought I could not put down," "it felt like"). Never launder a personal intuition into a stated fact, especially on contested ground.

## Banned constructions, with before/after examples

### The cadence cliché as ornament ("one at a time," "one by one," "one after another")

A writerly rhythm that, used decoratively, signals effort rather than meaning. Test: is the phrase load-bearing or ornamental? As the plain first statement of a literal image it can be exactly right. It bites when it dresses up a sentence that has nothing else to offer.

- **KEEP (load-bearing):** *The lights in the basilica went out one at a time.* (a literal event stated plainly — the plainness carries authority)
- **CUT (ornamental):** *One by one, the doubts fell away, one after another, until only faith remained.* (rhythm performing the work that meaning should do)

### The aphoristic both-sides loop ("X, because nobody Xes the Y they can already Z")

The sentence that folds back on itself to sound wise. It sounds composed by a machine optimizing for profundity.

- **BAD:** *...singing words I didn't examine, because nobody examines the words they can already sing.*
- **GOOD:** *...I had sung the liturgy for years without once stopping to ask what it meant.*

### Empty poetic abstraction

Images that sound evocative but denote nothing. If you cannot say what it means when pressed, cut it.

- **BAD:** *...Pentecostal services where the air came apart and people spoke in tongues.*
- **GOOD:** *...Pentecostal services where people spoke in tongues and were, by their own account, seized, taken up by something that used their bodies and then handed them back.*

Note what the good version does: it stays physical ("used their bodies," "handed them back"), marks the experience as reported rather than confirmed ("by their own account"), and gives the reader something to see.

### The dramatic reversal as a verbal tic ("Not X. Y.")

Powerful once, exhausting in repetition. Permit it rarely, for a genuine hinge. Ban it as a default rhythm.

- **OVERUSED:** *It was not loud. It was not soft. It was something else.*
- **EARNED (sparingly):** *Not is it true. Something stranger, and far more useful.*

### The rule of three on autopilot

Three is not a magic number. Use two when two is true, four when four is true. Triplets everywhere are a generator's reflex.

### Hedge and filler words

Cut "genuinely," "honestly," "actually," "truly," "simply" wherever they are doing emotional throat-clearing rather than work.

### Over-explaining the just-said

Do not state an idea and then restate it in case the reader missed it. Trust the first statement. The machine reflex is to gloss; resist it.

## Concrete before abstract, always

This is the rule that underlies all the others. Every abstraction needs a thing you could photograph.

**Weak (abstraction first):** *The convergence of these practices across cultures points to something deep in human nature.*

**Strong (concrete first):** *A string of counted beads for running a phrase in circles is the kind of thing you would expect one clever person to invent once. Instead it appears again and again, among peoples with no way to copy each other, because the practice it serves keeps being rediscovered.*

The abstraction ("something deep in human nature") is earned only after the beads. Lead with the beads.

Another example, grounding an abstract claim in a physical analogy:

**Weak:** *An actor can access genuine emotion without authentic belief.*

**Strong:** *An actor does not need to believe he is a grieving king to weep real tears on a stage, and the water on his face is real water, and the grief that tightens his chest is a real event in a real body.*

The concrete version is also longer, and that is fine. The length is doing work.

---

## Sentence rhythm

- Vary length deliberately. A long Huxley sentence should be followed or preceded by a short one that lets the reader breathe. Three long sentences in a row is fog; three short ones in a row is a telegram.
- End paragraphs on the strong beat, not the explanatory tail. Cut the sentence that explains the good last line.
- Read every paragraph aloud. If you run out of breath or lose the thread, it is too long or too tangled.

---

## Diction

- Prefer the concrete noun to the abstract one. "Granite," "candle," "knocking" before "architecture," "ritual," "atmosphere."
- Prefer the plain word to the elevated one unless the elevated word is exact. Reach for the precise rare word, never the merely fancy one.
- Numbers are allowed and good when specific and verified ("the vault at eighty-two feet," "a hundred and thirty-eight feet to the dome"). Vague magnitude ("incredibly tall," "massive") is weak.
- Avoid therapy-speak and wellness-speak ("holding space," "journey," "showing up for yourself"). The voice is older and cooler than that vocabulary.

## Positive patterns to reach for

### The plain-statement opening

A first line that states the central image as plainly as it can be stated, trusting the image to carry the weight without decoration. Plainness reads as authority.

> *The lights in the basilica went out one at a time.*

### The posture-planting sentence

One quiet, early sentence that tells the reader where the narrator stands, so they stop bracing for the piece they fear they are reading. Install the narrator's stance in a single unshowy line near the top.

> *...not hostile to religion and holding no particular opinion of the people who practiced it.*

### The set-up hinge before a list of effects

A short sentence that announces what the following sentences will be about, so the reader receives them in the right key. Cheap to include, expensive to omit.

> *I only knew what it did.* (followed by several sentences describing exactly what it did)

### The pull-back from particular to pattern

Establish a specific case in full concrete detail, then step back to show what it exemplifies. The Gladwell move. Do not reverse the order: pattern first, then example is weaker every time.

---

## The master test

Before any sentence stays: *Did a thoughtful person write this, or did a machine assemble it to sound like one?*

If there is any em dash, cadence cliché, empty image, self-folding aphorism, or unanchored abstraction, the answer is the second one, and the sentence gets rewritten.
